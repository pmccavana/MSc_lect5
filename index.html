Small Fields¶

Generally a beam can be considered to be small if lateral charged particle equilibrium is not established. (As a guide, this occurs if the radius of the field is less than d_max of the beam ).

Broad and narrow beam depth doses




Here we see the spectra from 10x10 and 1x1 fields.

Clearly there is a reduction in the low energy photons, they are not being scattered in and so the spectrum is harder.

This mean higher energy is reflected in the electron distribution, which is also affected by the lack of lower energies being scattered in.

The Output Factor $\Omega$ of small fields is not just the ratio of the reading of the small field detector at the two field sizes but must include a dosimetric correction , $k^{f_{clin},f_{msr}}_{Q_{clin},Q_{msr}}$, to include known deviations due to spectral or fluence changes

$$ \Omega^{f_{clin},f_{msr}}_{Q_{clin},Q_{msr}} = \frac{M^{f_{clin}}_{w,Q_{clin}}}{M^{f_{msr}}_{w,Q_{msr}}} k^{f_{clin},f_{msr}}_{Q_{clin},Q_{msr}} $$

Obtaining accurate dose measurements in small fields can be challenging. A typical approach is to perform measurements with different detectors and come to a concensus from the variety of results obtained.
